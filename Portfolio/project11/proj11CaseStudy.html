2 Weeks 2 Die

In this project, I spearheaded the UX design for a noir-themed murder mystery board game, guiding my team through the intricate process of crafting an immersive and intuitive user experience.

Overview

Objective

The objective of this project study was to create a board game from initial concept to final product over a semester. This case study will focuses on the detailed process of bringing an initial idea to life through stages of ideation, iterative design, user testing, and final production. This journey highlights how the game not only provides entertainment but also integrates advanced design principles and user experience insights.

My Role: Lead Designer

As the Lead Designer of this project, my role was pivotal in steering the board game's development from its conception to fruition. My responsibilities were twofold, encompassing both project leadership and hands-on UX design.

Outcome

The board game project was a resounding success, exceeding our initial expectations in both player engagement and design efficacy. This achievement is a testament to the effective application of UX principles, resulting in a product that is not only enjoyable but also user-friendly and aesthetically pleasing.

Understanding the Objective

Why are we doing this?

This board game project was initiated for two purposes: to innovate within the real of tabletop games, and to explore how UX principles can be effectively applies in a non-digital medium. To kick off the project, I collaborated with four other students to determine our key focus areas:

1. Understanding and accommodating the varied demographics of our players to ensure wide appeal and accessibility.

2. Creating game mechanics that retain user engagement and promote replability.

3. Concentrating on the game's potential to foster social interaction and teamwork.

Research Methods

Playtesting

Throughout the development of the board game, I conducted several iterations of playtesting, each designed to assess and enhance player interaction and comprehension.

In light of the game's dual-role structure, where players assume either a murderer or a detective, I implemented targeted surveys for each role. This approach ensured that the feedback gathered was role-specific and relevant, allowing me to fine tune the game experience to suit each unique perspective.

For investigators we focused on:

  • The clarity of the evidence's information
  • The process of gathering clues
  • The extent of their collaboration with others

For the murderer we focused on:

  • The balance of the game mechanics
  • Impact of stealth and deception
  • Their strategy and tactics

Defining the Problem

Insights

After our first round of playtesting, I collected the results and regrouped with my team. I was able to deduce trends and draw insights from the surveys. Heres some of the key insights included listed below.

Users felt the game was too long.

Players consistently reported that the game's duration was excessively long, diminishing the overall enjoyment. The structure of the game led to all clue cards being collected before reaching the midpoint, disrupting the pace and tension crucial to the experience.

Clue and evidence card timing was too late.

Feedback highlighted a significant issue with the timing of evidence cards' introduction into gameplay. Their late appearance often left players unable to solve the case, leading to frustration and a sense of unfulfillment.

Murderer's role was inefficient

A notable concern was the limited active engagement of the player in the murderer role. The current game design restricts the murderer's actions due to high point requirements for activities, leading to a less enjoyable experience.

Design

We clearly had our work cut out for us. The goals was to now brainstorm solutions about how to remove the lack of engagement both parties felt. I also recieved feedback from my professor before moving on to drafting some enhancments.

Phase 1: Addressing Initial Playtest Feedback.

Early Introduction of Evidence Cards: To avoid the issue of players being unable to solve the case due to late presentation of evidence cards, I adjusted the game mechanics to introduce these cards earlier.

Clarifying Game Rules and Objectives: I revised the game's instructions and rulebook to clearly state the hidden role of the murderer, the significance of red spots for movement, and procedural details like the order of play and the collaborative nature of investigators.

Improving Clue Relevance and Distribution: Realizing that clues were too vague and were all picked up too quickly, I rebalanced the number and nature of clues and red herrings to ensure a more challenging and engaging deduction process.

Phase 2: Enhancing the Role of the Murderer.

Empowering the Murderer: Feedback indicated the murderer's role was too passive. I addressed this by reducing the points required for actions, enabling more dynamic and strategic play for this role.

Reconfiguring the Map: Noting that the murderer was confined to a small section, I reconfigured the game map to allow greater mobility and interaction possibilities for the murderer.

My role as the Lead Designer in facilitating multiple rounds of playtesting, analyzing feedback, and then implementing changes based on that feedback, really drove home the value of iterative design.
